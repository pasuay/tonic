Why this works

Each round plays a short I–IV–V–I cadence to plant a key in your ear, then a single note. Your job is to name its function (do, re, mi…) relative to that key — not the interval from the last note. Re-hearing the key every round stops you slipping into interval-chaining, which is the habit that leaves you needing an instrument to find "do".1

After each answer, the note resolves down (or up) to the tonic through the real scale degrees — fa→mi→re→do, ti→do — so you hear where it wants to go. Sounding a degree and letting it settle to "do" is the core of the movable-do / Kodály tradition for building the inner ear.2 Stages are introduced in order, simplest first, which mirrors how that tradition sequences learning.3

The pad colors encode tonal tension, not absolute pitch: the stable tonic-triad degrees (do, mi, so) glow warm; the restless tendency tones (fa, ti) glow cool. This is a learning scaffold with real precedent, but note-colour is idiosyncratic in actual synesthetes and won't give you absolute pitch. So the color fades as you advance: full in Stage 1, gone by the later stages, forcing you to hear function without the visual prop.

Find do removes the cadence entirely: a short melody plays cold and you must infer the key from the melody alone before answering. That inference draws on the tonal hierarchy your ear already carries — the well-replicated finding that listeners internalise which tones are stable in a key and can locate a tonic from just a few notes of context.4

Recognize vs. Sing-back. Clicking a name trains sound→name (recognition). Sing-back flips it: the app names a degree, you sing that pitch against the key, and the mic checks how close you land. That trains name→sound (production) — the direction you use when you're playing and need to find a note. It grades your pitch, in any octave, not the word you sang. Together the two modes wire both directions of the link.

Practise a little every day. That habit, not any single feature, is what the research consistently points to.
